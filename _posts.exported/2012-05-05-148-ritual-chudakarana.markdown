<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal;"><span style="font-family: verdana,geneva; color: #000000;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">The Chudakarana (<span class="unicode">Cū</span><span class="unicode">ḍ</span><span class="unicode">ākara</span><span class="unicode">ṇ</span><span class="unicode">a</span>) (literally, arrangement of the hair tuft) or the Mundana (literally, tonsure), is the eighth of the sixteen <span style="text-decoration: none;">Hindu</span><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sa%E1%B9%83sk%C4%81ra" title="Saṃskāra"><span style="color: #000000;"><span style="color: windowtext; text-decoration: none;"></span><span style="color: windowtext; text-decoration: none;"></span><span style="color: windowtext; text-decoration: none;"></span></span></a> (sacraments), in which a child receives his/her <span style="text-decoration: none;">first haircut</span>.</span></span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: verdana,geneva; font-size: 12pt; color: #000000;">According to the <span style="text-decoration: none;">Grhya Sutras</span>, this samskara should take place at the end of first year or before the expiry of the third year, but the later authorities extend the age to the seventh year.<sup id="cite_ref-0"> </sup>The child’s hair is shorn, frequently leaving only the <span style="text-decoration: none;">śikhā</span> or cūḍā, a tuft at the crown of the head.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: verdana,geneva; font-size: 12pt; color: #000000;">Originally, the arrangement of the śikhā was the most significant feature of the Chudakarana and the number of tufts was determined by the number of the <span style="text-decoration: none;">pravaras</span> belonging to the <span style="text-decoration: none;">gotra</span> of the child. Later, in northern <span style="text-decoration: none;">I</span><span style="text-decoration: none;">ndia</span>, keeping only one tuft became universal. But in the <span style="text-decoration: none;">Deccan</span> and southern India, earlier traditions remained alive to some extent.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: verdana,geneva; font-size: 12pt; color: #000000;">In tradition, the hair from birth is associated with undesirable traits from past lives. Thus at the time of the mundan, the child is freshly shaven to signify freedom from the past and moving into the future. The rite is performed as a special ceremony in most homes, for young girls and boys.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: verdana,geneva; font-size: 12pt; color: #000000;">At <span style="text-decoration: none;">Rishikesh</span>, on the banks of the <span style="text-decoration: none;">Ganges</span>, there is a special chudakarana or mundana samskara. In this ceremony, along with cutting and shaving hair, <span style="text-decoration: none;">Vedic</span> <span style="text-decoration: none;">mantras</span> and prayers are chanted by trained priests, <span style="text-decoration: none;">acharyas</span> and <span style="text-decoration: none;">rishikumaras</span>. The child's head is shaven and the hair is then symbolically offered to the holy river. The child and his/her family then perform a sacred <span style="text-decoration: none;">yajna</span> ceremony and the <span style="text-decoration: none;">Ganga Aarti</span>.</span></p>
