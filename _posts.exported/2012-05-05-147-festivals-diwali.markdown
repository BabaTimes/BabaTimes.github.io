<p><span style="font-family: verdana,geneva; font-size: 12pt; color: #000000;">Diwali (also spelled Devali in certain regions) or Deepavali,<sup id="cite_ref-0"> </sup>popularly known as the "festival of lights," is a <span style="text-decoration: none;">festival</span> celebrated between mid-October and mid-December for different reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-1"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diwali#cite_note-1"><span style="color: #000000; text-decoration: none;"></span></a></sup>For <span style="text-decoration: none;">Hindus</span>, Diwali is one of the most important festivals of the year and is celebrated in families by performing traditional activities together in their homes. For <span style="text-decoration: none;">Jains</span>, Diwali marks the attainment of <span style="text-decoration: none;">moksha</span> or <span style="text-decoration: none;">nirvana</span> by <span style="text-decoration: none;">Mahavira</span> in 527 BCE.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: verdana,geneva; font-size: 12pt; color: #000000;">Diwali is an official holiday in <span style="text-decoration: none;">India</span>,<sup id="cite_ref-4"> </sup><span style="text-decoration: none;">Nepal</span>, <span style="text-decoration: none;">Sri Lanka</span>, <span style="text-decoration: none;">Myanmar</span>, <span style="text-decoration: none;">Mauritius</span>, <span style="text-decoration: none;">Guyana</span>, <span style="text-decoration: none;">Trinidad &amp; Tobago</span>, <span style="text-decoration: none;">Suriname</span>, <span style="text-decoration: none;">Malaysia</span>, <span style="text-decoration: none;">Singapore</span>,<sup id="cite_ref-5"> </sup>and <span style="text-decoration: none;">Fiji</span>. The name "Diwali" is a contraction of "Deepavali" (<span class="unicode">Dīpāvalī</span>), which translates into "row of lamps". Diwali involves the lighting of small clay lamps (<span style="text-decoration: none;">diyas</span> or dīpas) in filled with oil to signify the triumph of good over evil.<sup id="cite_ref-Demon_8-0"> </sup>These lamps are kept on during the night and one's house is cleaned, both done in order to make the goddess <span style="text-decoration: none;">Lakshmi</span> feel welcome.<sup id="cite_ref-Raat_9-0"> </sup><span style="text-decoration: none;">Firecrackers</span> are burst in order to drive away <span style="text-decoration: none;">evil spirits</span>. During Diwali, all the celebrants wear new clothes and share <span style="text-decoration: none;">sweets</span> and snacks with family members and friends.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: verdana,geneva; font-size: 12pt; color: #000000;">The festival starts with <span style="text-decoration: none;">Dhanteras</span> on which most Indian business communities begin their financial year. The second day of the festival, <span style="text-decoration: none;">Naraka Chaturdasi</span>, marks the vanquishing of the demon <span style="text-decoration: none;">Naraka</span> by <span style="text-decoration: none;">Lord Krishna</span> and his wife <span style="text-decoration: none;">Satyabhama</span>. Amavasya, the third day of Diwali, marks the worship of <span style="text-decoration: none;">Lakshmi</span>, the goddess of wealth in her most benevolent mood, fulfilling the wishes of her devotees. Amavasya also tells the story of Lord <span style="text-decoration: none;">Vishnu</span>, who in his dwarf incarnation vanquished the <span style="text-decoration: none;">Bali</span>, and banished him to <span style="text-decoration: none;">Patala</span>. It is on the fourth day of Diwali, Kartika Shudda Padyami, that Bali went to <span style="text-decoration: none;">patala</span> and took the reins of his new kingdom in there. The fifth day is referred to as Yama Dvitiya (also called Bhai Dooj), and on this day sisters invite their brothers to their homes.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: verdana,geneva; font-size: 12pt; color: #000000;">It begins in late <span style="text-decoration: none;">Ashvin</span> (between September and October) and ends in early <span style="text-decoration: none;">Kartika</span> (between October and November). The days in Ashvin are in the <span style="text-decoration: none;">Krishna Paksha</span> ("dark fortnight") of that month, while the days in Kartik are in its <span style="text-decoration: none;">Shukla Paksha</span> ("bright fortnight"). The first day is Dhan Teras. The last day is Yama Dvitiya, which signifies the second day of the light half of Kartika. Each day of Diwali marks one celebration of the six principal stories associated with the festival.</span></p>
